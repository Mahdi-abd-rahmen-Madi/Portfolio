Hardening non-custodial wallets : A beginner guide to safeguard your digital assets!

Abstract:

Securing non-custodial wallets is an essential step in protecting your cryptocurrency assets. Non-custodial wallets provide a high level of security by giving users control over their private keys and not relying on a third party to hold their assets. However, non-custodial wallets are still vulnerable to certain types of risks, and it is important to take steps to secure them

This in-depth article explains how each type of attack works and provides guidance on how to protect against them. Stay informed, proactive and keep your assets SAFU.

Introduction :

There are three categories of attacks that someone could use to steal your crypto :

  • Private key vulnerabilities,
  • Machine vulnerabilities,
  • Smart contract vulnerabilities.   

And while using a hardware wallet in conjunction with a non-custodial wallet client may seem the way to go, it only defends against a small percentage of potential attacks (attack #3 and attack 6).

Now, let's talk about each specific attack and defense.

A. Private key vulnerabilities:

Someone with your private key can steal all your money from anywhere in the world, this results in a complete loss of funds.

So what are the ways someone can get a hold of your secret recovery phrase or private key?

Attack #1 Private Key Phishing

Private key phishing attacks typically involve the attacker sending the victim a fake email or message that appears to be from a legitimate cryptocurrency exchange or a wallet provider.

The message typically includes a link that directs the victim to a phishing website that mimics the authentic one.

Once the victim enters their private key on the fake site, the attacker can then use it to access their wallet and steal their assets.

How to defend against phishing attack ?

Never reveal your secret phrase or private key (screen sharing sessions, shoulder surfing…)

The only time you're going to use your passphrase or private keys is when setting up your wallet on a new device or recovering a lost password.

Attack #2 Unsecure Phrase Storage :

The files on your computer are inherently unsecure by design, Malware, and computer viruses are far too common, nowadays, and scammers are becoming more sophisticated in elaborating their attack vectors.

Unsecure phrase storage refers to the practice of storing sensitive information, such as password phrases or private keys, in an unsecure way,

This could include :

  • Storing the information on a device that is not password protected or compromised by malware.
  • Writing it down on a piece of paper and leaving it in a visible location.
  • Sharing it with others. (Willingly or unintentionally)

So, what is your defense ?

  • Don't store your recovery phrase digitally in an unencrypted format (screenshots, e-mails to yourself or text files) as it may be vulnerable to cyberattacks
  • Don't store your recovery phrase in an online password managers can be vulnerable to data breaches.
  • Use a secure recovery phrase generator, such as a hardware device that generates and stores the recovery phrase offline (Ledger, Trezor...) or create your own with Airgap Vault.
  • Write it down on a piece of paper, laminate that paper to keep it waterproof, then fragment it and store it separately in different locations using Shamir Secret Sharing Scheme. (Create your own here)
  • Use an offline password manager such as KeepassXC

Attack #3 Stolen Encrypted Private Key

Some types of malware (usually rootkits or Trojans) that allows the attacker to download files stored locally on your computer. Therefore, downloading the encrypted private key.

The private key, which is stored locally, is encrypted with your Metamask password. So, for an attacker to decrypt and read the file, they would need your password. This can be obtained through a brute force attack.

Keyloggers :

Another type of malware that logs and records everything you type on your keyboard, making it easy for attackers to steal your password by sifting through your keyboard history.

How to defend against brute force attacks ?

Use a good password that contains random digits, symbols, and upper and lower case characters.

That way, Even if someone is able to get a hold of your encrypted private key, it will be difficult for them to unlock it.

Even though a strong password can protect your device, it cannot prevent keyloggers from recording your password as you type it.

In order to protect against losing your encrypted private key, you need to protect against malware.
We'll talk more about that in the next section.

The most effective way to protect against stolen encrypted private key is to avoid storing it on your computer or phone, which is the default storing mechanism for non-custodial wallets.

So the best of both worlds would be able to still use wallet provider as your front end, but bypass its default key storage mechanism.

Why should you use a hardware wallet ?

A hardware wallet is a sophisticated USB stick that separates your private key from your computer or phone. It is cleverly designed to handle your private key in such a way that the key never exits the device, even when used to sign transactions.

And the great news is that you can still use your favorite non-custodial wallet (Metamask, Trust wallet …) with full functionality as your front-end user interface, while bypassing the default key storage mechanism.

This concept is what is referred to as a hot wallet and cold wallet.

Cold wallet VS hot wallet, what's the difference?

Hot wallets have keys stored on devices connected to the internet, and therefore vulnerable to malware, and cold wallets have private keys generated and stored on devices that are not connected to the internet.

Hardware wallets are considered the safest option for protecting against Stolen Encrypted Private Key, but there is one area where they should not be trusted, which is called:

Attack#4 Supply Chain Attack :

A supply chain attack is a type of cyber attack in which the attacker targets a vulnerable point in the supply chain of a company in order to gain access to the company's network or steal sensitive information. This can occur at any point along the supply chain, including during the design, development, production, delivery, or maintenance stages. Supply chain attacks can also be used to distribute fake products or real products with a software that has been tampered with. Or it can come with instructions that lead you to use an old wallet with a private key that someone else already owns.

How to defend against supply chain attacks ?

To ensure the security of your assets, purchase a hardware wallet directly from a reputable company and avoid buying a used one.

Unfortunately, Many individuals often inadvertently create a hot wallet as a result of improperly configuring their hardware wallets when linking it to Metamask.

B) machine vulnerabilities.

Attack #5 Clipboard Hijacking

Clipboard hijacking is a malware that changes the contents of your copy and paste clipboard.

This type of malware is only active when you copy a wallet address, it will change your clipboard contents to a different address (the attacker address)

Luckily, your defense here is simple.

  • Always verify your transaction details before confirming.
  • Double-check the address you are sending to, and make sure that it also matches the address you were intending to send to.
  • Always do a test transaction before sending large amounts (send $1 worth of money as a test)

User errors are the biggest reason people lose money in crypto, and test transactions are the most beautifully simple way to avoid that.

Attack #6 Malware in General:

The truth is, malwares are evolving at a faster pace than cybersecurity experts can keep up with.

So what can you do about avoiding malware in general?

To protect oneself from online threats in the crypto space, it is important to take a multi-layered approach:

  • First, exercise caution and maintain safe internet habits, such as avoiding suspicious websites and links.
  • Second, consider using a hardware wallet for added security against malware.
  • Finally, the most secure option is to use a dedicated device specifically for crypto transactions and to keep it separate from other work or personal computing devices."

Using crypto and DeFi, particularly with self-custodial wallets like MetaMask, involves inherent risks.

However, the risk of infinite token approvals is something you shouldn't tolerate. You may not even know you're accepting it though because the default action is also the riskiest one.

This brings us to section number three:

C) Smart Contract Vulnerabilities:

Attack #7 Infinite Token Approvals:

Infinite token approval is a mechanism that enables a smart contract to authorize an unlimited number of tokens for transfer on the behalf of the contract owner. This mechanism is frequently used in decentralized finance (DeFi) applications such as lending and borrowing platforms to ensure seamless token transfer without requiring the contract owner to approve each transaction individually.

With infinite token approval, the smart contract has the ability to automatically authorize any token transfer up to a limit established by the contract owner.

Approving an infinite number of token may seem convenient, (reduced cost on gas fees) However, it introduces a security concern:
if the contract is compromised, an attacker could potentially drain the entire approved balance even if you use a hardware wallet.

Luckily, the scope of this attack is limited as it only targets the tokens that have been approved for infinite use and not the entire wallet.

To mitigate this risk:

Don't do infinite token approvals, it is recommended to only approve a reasonable limit and regularly review and adjust the approved amount.

This will greatly reduce your risk for future exposure if the smart contract get hacked.

To change the default unlimited approvals (on Metamask) :

Click View Full Transaction Details > Edit > Custom Spend Limit
then, enter the number of tokens you want to approve, and click Save.

But if you unknowingly granted infinite token approvals, How to revoke approvals ?

One way to check and revoke your approvals is by using a tool called DeBank. Essentially, you are simply issuing a new approval with a value of zero, rather than infinite, to the contract using DeBank. [requires the entry of the seed phrase]

Alternatively, you can use other blockchain explorers such as PolygonScan, BSCScan, and EtherScan, which also have an approval section to perform the same action. [Recommended]

The choice between a limited and infinite approval is a compromise between simplicity and security.

Attack #8 Smart Contract Hacks

Smart contracts, the backbone of DeFi, offer great opportunities but also pose a significant security challenge :

A smart contract hack refers to an exploit or vulnerability in the code of a smart contract that allows an attacker to gain unauthorized access or perform unauthorized actions on the contract. This can include things like stealing funds from the contract, modifying the contract's logic, or creating a denial of service attack. Smart contract hacks can occur due to bugs in the contract code, poor programming practices, or a lack of proper security testing.

A compromised smart contract can result in the loss of all funds currently staked within it. It can be either deliberately malicious or unintentionally flawed, either by having a bug or vulnerability in it.

So, how can you defend against the inherent risks of using smart contracts?

DeFi Insurance is the simplest solution, and it will likely become more prevalent in the future.

However, it's currently not widely adopted. The DeFi insurance market is still in its infancy, with only a few options available, which don't cover most protocols. Nevertheless, it's worth considering as DeFi continues to evolve.

Or make your own risk assessment, to do that consider:

  • Well-established protocols with multiple audits and a proven long track record. (Use Dex Screener)
  • Read the audits, including any issues found, their severity, and current status.

A well-audited project should have resolved significant issues identified in the audit, while a flawed protocol often has acknowledged but unresolved major or medium risks

  • Be cautious of protocols that advertise having an audit, even if they have serious security flaws.
  • Use RUGDoc for initial security research.

Note : RUGDoc is not an auditing service, but it provides an initial security assessment of the code

  • Learn about past exploits through rect.news.

Now, let's talk about a non-attack, (Disconnecting MetaMask Does Nothing)

A non-attack is a security misconception that provides no benefits.

Many people around the crypto sphere recommends that you disconnect your wallet from a DApp (Decentralised app) after using it. This is false!

Connecting your wallet to a Dapp does, only, exposes your public wallet address. Thus, disconnecting does not add any additional security.

The only potential actions someone can take with your public address are legitimate airdrops or scam token phishing attempts which we will cover about in the final section :

Attack #9 Scam Tokens

Scam tokens are crypto assets or digital tokens that are created and promoted by fraudsters with the intention of scamming people out of their money. Scammers typically create a fake blockchain project, website, and whitepaper, and then market it as a legitimate investment opportunity.

They'll use your excitement or curiosity over the fact that you think you got free money (airdrops) to encourage you to go to their website.

Their website will probably be an attempt to get you to interact with their malicious smart contracts (attack #8) or a way to phish your secret recovery phrase out of you (attack #1).

Your defense is:

Do not touch the tokens. They can't hurt you if you leave them alone.

Conclusion : The importance of owning your private keys:

Self-custodial wallets provide the purest form of ownership, but also the greatest risk, as you bear full responsibility for the security of your funds.

If you lose your money, it cannot be recovered by anyone, including law enforcement.

Inheritance planning is also an important consideration, This can include designating a beneficiary or executor to manage the assets, creating a will or trust, and providing specific instructions for how to access and distribute the assets.
