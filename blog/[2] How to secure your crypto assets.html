Introduction: What is a Digital Asset?

According to The Balance, Digital assets are anything that can be stored and transmitted electronically through a computer or other digital device, and are associated with ownership or use rights. Well-known examples today include cryptocurrencies and non-fungible tokens (NFTs). While you can’t hold digital assets with your hands, they are real assets that you can buy, sell, store, and trade online.

Types of Digital Assets and How to Protect Them ?

Digital assets are intangible assets that exist in cyberspace. They can be anything from a song to a bitcoin. Digital assets have been around for a while now, but they are only becoming more popular as the internet is becoming more and more pervasive in our lives. There are many ways to protect digital assets, but the most important thing is to know what you’re protecting and how you can protect it.

Why You Should Secure Your Digital Assets Today ?

The digital era has changed the way we store and share data. The internet has made it so easy to access, store, and share information that we often forget about the importance of protecting our data. But with so much sensitive information stored digitally, it’s essential to take steps to protect your digital assets and make sure they are safe from theft or hacking.

This article will provide you with some tips on how to secure your digital assets, as well as how to properly fortify your exchange accounts.

How do cybercriminals elaborate their attack vector ?

1) Social engineering attacks :

The first step in protecting yourself against social engineering attacks is recognizing them for what they are:

Social engineering attacks are attempts by hackers to gather information about you or your company by pretending to be someone else. They might pretend that they’re from your bank or a government agency, for example, in order to get you to reveal your password or other personal information.

Suppose you have your crypto on coinbase.com, How do you access your funds there? Well, you need 3 pieces of information, your username, your password, and the two-factor authentication code.

So let’s talk about how they’re doing that!

Email address:

Usually, hackers will scour through past database dumps such as the ledger database, and they’ll usually try to create a new exchange account with each and every email address.

The already created account will prompt that those email address have already being used for Sign Up, Now they have one third of the puzzle solved!.

Passwords:

Next, they’ll start going to past database breaches, looking for your email to see if it was in a breach that had your password in it, not necessarily Coinbase database dump, but any other site that you had registered with. And if they can find a password of yours that was in some other data breach, they’ll grab that and check if that’s also the same password you use on your Coinbase account. Often time, this works because a lot of people reuse the same password for every site they have a login for. So don’t reuse passwords ever.

But if they can’t find your password this way, they can get into your email, then tell Coinbase to reset the password, which will email you with a password reset link, so they can bypass the password all together.

So this is what they’ll focus on trying to guess or reset your email password. They might try hacking (by brute forcing) or social engineering your email provider to get into your account. And if that doesn’t work, they might try sim swapping you.

2) What is SIM Swapping ?

SIM swapping is the act of hijacking your phone number to either reset your email passwords, and from there, cybercriminals can reset your exchange account password, or approve malicious transactions via the SMS 2 factor-authentications code. And with that, they’ll work quickly to take all your funds and transfer it to an external wallet where you’ll never see that crypto again.

3) Phishing attacks :

According to Cloudflare, Phishing attacks refers to the act of luring victims, In an attempt to steal sensitive information, typically in the form of usernames, passwords, credit card numbers, bank account information etc…

By masquerading as a reputable source with an enticing request, In order to utilize or sell the stolen information, Similarly to how a fisherman uses bait to catch a fish.

So, how do you as an End-User defend against this?

Quick tips:

for email:

  • Use a unique email address just for your exchange or high security accounts. You should make the email itself hard for people to find!.
  • You can try going to haveibeenpwned and type in your email to see what sites have leaked your data.
  • Having a clean email address that isn’t used anywhere else makes it much harder for attackers to find.

For passwords:

  • Use a random password Generator from A FOSS Cross-platform Password Manager such as KeepassXC
  • Don’t reuse passwords, especially those that you use on your crypto exchange.

For 2 factor-authentications:

  • Don’t use SMS based two-factor authentication, Instead, use something like KeePassXC TOTP, Binance Authenticator or Google authenticator.
  • Be aware of the kind of damage that a sim swap can cause you, and take steps needed to protect against it.
  • Store your digital assets in a hardware wallet like Ledger or Trezor and lock it up in a safe.

Important!

Secure your exchange accounts even if they’re empty, those account can be linked to your bank account, so when maliciously accessed, third parties can buy crypto on your behalf and deposit them into their own wallets.

Note that you either need a Private Key or a Seed Phrase to get control of a wallet, So never ever let the seed phrase or the Private Key get out of your hands.

Don’t Store them digitally anywhere, Neither, in the clouds, nor emails to yourself.

The only place to ever enter them is in your wallet when you are setting up your wallet for the first time. Never ever give it to anyone or enter it into any website ever.

I personally recommend having 2 or more wallets, one as a long term vault, and another one for minting or everyday use.

The everyday use one should have a small amount of crypto (less than $300 worth of assets).

The long term vault should be a hardware wallet (Trezor or Ledger), where you store your long term holdings, and this one should only be used to send your crypto assets to and from your other wallets.

What Happens When You Don’t Protect Your Digital Assets?

In this era of globalization and digitization, it is inevitable that our digital assets will be exposed to different risks. In some cases, these risks can be managed by taking simple precautions such as using clean email addresses or even using a Password Manager for strong password generation and 2FA setups. But even with these precautions, there is always a chance of data being lost or stolen.

As we become more reliant on technology, the risk of data being lost or stolen increases. With one third of Americans reporting that they have had their information hacked in the last year, it’s not surprising to see that many companies are hesitant to store sensitive information online.
